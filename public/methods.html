Electrical resistance survey

Resistivity method uses a set of four electrodes to locally measure the ground electrical resistivity. Two electrodes are used to inject electricity in the soil and the two others measure a potential difference. Dividing the potential difference by the known amount of current gives a resistance. Archeological structure showing contrasting resistivity (masconery, ditches) can be revealed by this method.

Magnetomery

Magnetometry measures the small perturbations in the Earth magnetic field caused by burned or turned over ground. Usually two magnometers (device measuring the magnetic fields) are positioned either vertically or horizontally and the difference in magnetic field between the two sensors is computed. This arrangement is called a gradiometer and is often used in archeology.

Frequency domain electromagnetic induction

The instrument contains one transmitter coil and a few receiver coils. The transmitter coils emits an primary alternating electromagnetic field which induces eddy currents in the ground. These eddy currents generates a secondary magnetic field. Both primary and secondary magnetic fields are measured by the receiver coils. From their ration, it is possible to compute the ground electrical resistivity and the magnetic susceptibility. This technique can be used to map conductive anomalies (such as buried pipes, channels).

Ground penetrating radar

This method uses radar waves send to the ground from a transmitting antenna. The waves are partially reflected when encountering change in the ground permittivity. This method is useful to detect buried objects with a contrasting permittivity such as masconery structure or ditches. Set of 2D transect can be combined to form a 3D volume that can be slice at different depths. The instrument uses in this study was operating at 500 MHz and has a penetration depth of a few meters.
